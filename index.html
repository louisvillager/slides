Rashomon (1950)

"Rashomon" has such a clever storytelling technique, it inspired its own name for a psychological effect . It employs not one, but four unreliable narrators recounting the murder of a samurai by a simple roadside bandit. Each version is full of details that seem clearly embellished, but also some that are quite plausible. In the end, the viewer is left with ambiguity, allowing them to choose the most correct version for themselves.

Seven Samurai (1954)

This is Kurosawa's magnum opus, a 3-hour-long (yup) paragon of the samurai genre. Perhaps you've seen the western ripoff remake, "The Magnificent Seven". (The first one, with Yul Brynner and Steve McQueen. I can't vouch for the new one with Denzel and Andy from "Parks and Recreation".)

In this tale, a poor farming village under the thumb of bandits acts on an ambitious plan of liberation by hiring samurai to defeat their oppressors. Those warriors who answer the call range from a seasoned and wise samurai, to a starry-eyed neophyte, and even an illiterate thief who happens to more or less be good with a blade, to name a few. The epic struggle shows these samurai, would-be samurai, and farmers using every means they can they can think of to free their village from the more numerous and better-equipped bandits.

Throne of Blood (1957)

Have you ever watched Macbeth and thought, "Sure, it's compelling, but I wonder how it would look when told through the lens of traditional Japanese Noh theater"? Well, good news! Kurosawa has just the movie for you.

So, it's pretty much Macbeth. Taketoki Washizu receives a prophecy from a witch foretelling his rise to power. Before long, he becomes obsessed with this prophecy and the pursuit of power for power's sake, driving him and his wife, the Lady Asaji Washizu, to ever-greater lengths (and depths) to attain it. However, they both learn the true cost of ambition in the end.

The Hidden Fortress (1958)

"The Hidden Fortress" is the story of a princess and her bodyguard/general who must sneak across enemy lines to the safety of a neighboring kingdom. They recruit the help of two peasant soldiers who are more motivated by the promise of a large reward of gold than any greater struggle.

This movie may be perhaps best known as one of the biggest inspirations for "Star Wars: Episode IV - A New Hope". George Lucas particularly singled out how the story was told not through the perspective of the princess or her general, but rather "through the eyes of two lesser characters"—in this case, the two peasants.

Yojinbo (1961)

My personal favorite, and yes, I will be obnoxious and use the correct Japanese spelling of Yojinbo and not Yojimbo. I don't care what the official title is. The "m" is a vestige of an outdated style of Romanizing Japanese words.

"Yojinbo" is the story of a nameless ronin who frees a town from two rival warlords by tricking them into fighting each other. You may have already seen the Sergio Leone western remake, "A Fistfull of Dollars", starring Clint Eastwood. "Yojinbo" has clever humor and interesting characters, and it features a hero who relies on his wit far more than his sword. If you're looking to try your first samurai film, I highly recommend starting here.

Sanjuro (1962)

The unofficial sequel, of sorts, to "Yojinbo", "Sanjuro" follows the same character from the previous film as he thwarts a corrupt clan superintendent from framing a rival for his own abuses. Just as in the other film, he relies on outwitting the more numerous foe, with a fair bit of humor along the way.

Truth be told, this is the movie on this list that I remember the least about. I had to reference IMDb for the particulars of the plot. It's not a bad film by any means, but the other entrants on this list are just that much more memorable. (Oddly, despite "Sanjuro" functioning as the sequel to "Yojinbo", Tatsuya Nakadai is in both films, playing different characters, each one of whom dies in their respective movies.)
